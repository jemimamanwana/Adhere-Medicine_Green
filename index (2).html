Understanding Diabetes

What is Diabetes?

Diabetes is a chronic condition that occurs when blood glucose (blood sugar) is too high. It develops when the pancreas fails to produce enough insulin, produces no insulin at all, or when the body does not respond properly to the insulin it produces.

75% of diabetics can achieve better control through adherence

Medication adherence means regularly taking your prescribed medications — such as metformin or insulin — as instructed, at the right time, in the correct dose, while following specific instructions like taking medicine with or without food, and continuing treatment for the recommended period.

For young adults, this also includes maintaining a healthy diet and regularly monitoring blood glucose levels. When done consistently, adherence helps manage diabetes effectively and prevent serious long-term complications.

Classification

Types of Diabetes

Not all diabetes is the same. Understanding which type you have shapes your treatment plan and adherence strategy.

🛡️
Type 1

An autoimmune disease where the immune system attacks and destroys insulin-producing beta cells in the pancreas. The body produces little to no insulin. Requires lifelong insulin therapy.

Autoimmune
⚖️
Type 2

The most common type, where the body does not produce enough insulin or the cells do not respond properly to insulin (insulin resistance). Strongly linked to lifestyle factors.

Lifestyle-linked
🤰
Gestational

Occurs during pregnancy when placental hormones cause insulin resistance. Usually resolves after birth but increases the mother's risk of developing Type 2 diabetes later in life.

Pregnancy-related

Less common types include Type 3c (caused by damage to the pancreas from injury or disease) and neonatal diabetes (a rare form diagnosed in infants). All types require careful medication management.

Why Adherence Is Critical

The Cost of Non-Adherence

Skipping medication might feel harmless day-to-day. Over time, uncontrolled blood glucose silently damages vital organs and systems throughout your body.

❤️
Cardiovascular Disease
High blood glucose damages blood vessel walls, increasing the risk of stroke, heart attack, and peripheral artery disease. Cardiovascular disease is the leading cause of death in people with diabetes.
🫘
Kidney Damage (Nephropathy)
Prolonged hyperglycaemia damages the tiny blood vessels in the kidneys, reducing their ability to filter waste. This can progress to chronic kidney disease and may require dialysis.
👁️
Eye Damage (Retinopathy)
Damage to the blood vessels in the retina can cause blurred vision and, if untreated, permanent blindness. Diabetes is one of the leading causes of preventable blindness worldwide.
⚡
Nerve Damage (Neuropathy)
Normal glucose levels prevent nerve damage, which often manifests as tingling, numbness, or burning pain — usually starting in the hands and feet. Severe cases can cause loss of feeling entirely.
🧠
Cognitive Decline
Evidence links long-term poor glycaemic control to accelerated cognitive decline, increased risk of dementia, and brain structural changes — even in younger adults.
🦶
Foot Damage & Amputation
Combined neuropathy and poor circulation can cause foot ulcers that heal poorly. In severe cases, infection leads to gangrene and amputation. Diabetes is the most common cause of non-traumatic lower limb amputations.

⚠️ Acute Emergencies

Beyond long-term complications, poor medication adherence can trigger sudden, life-threatening crises that require immediate hospitalisation.

"Diabetic Ketoacidosis (DKA) occurs when the body lacks enough insulin to let blood sugar enter cells for energy. The body begins burning fat instead, producing ketones — triggering dangerous acidity of the blood."

Symptoms of DKA include extreme thirst, frequent urination, nausea, vomiting, abdominal pain, and confusion. It is a medical emergency.

Sources: WHO — Diabetes Fact Sheet · IDF — Diabetes Complications

The Core Case

Why Medication Adherence Matters

Consistent medication use is not optional for diabetes management — it is the foundation on which every other health outcome rests.

01
Maintains Normal Blood Glucose
Adherence ensures medication levels remain consistent in the bloodstream, essential for maintaining blood sugar within a healthy range. Taking medication as prescribed prevents both hypoglycaemia (dangerous lows) and chronic hyperglycaemia (damaging highs).
02
Prevents Vascular Complications
Microvascular complications involve damage to small blood vessels. Chronic hyperglycaemia causes these vessels to become weak and leak — leading to retinopathy, nephropathy, and neuropathy. Consistent adherence protects every blood vessel in your body.
03
Avoids Acute Emergencies
Poor adherence can rapidly trigger life-threatening conditions such as Diabetic Ketoacidosis (DKA) or Hyperosmolar Hyperglycaemic State (HHS), requiring emergency hospitalisation. Consistent medication prevents these dangerous crises entirely.
04
Reduces Long-term Healthcare Costs
While medication has a cost, the financial burden of treating complications — hospitalisations, dialysis, eye surgery, amputations — far exceeds the cost of consistent adherence. Staying on track today saves enormous expense tomorrow.
05
Preserves Quality of Life
Uncontrolled diabetes affects energy, cognition, mood, and physical capacity. Adherence keeps symptoms manageable, allowing young adults to study, work, exercise, and socialise without constant interference from their condition.
06
Slows Disease Progression
Research consistently shows that maintaining HbA1c levels through adherence slows or prevents the progression from manageable diabetes to severe complications. Early consistency pays the greatest dividends in long-term health outcomes.

Why This Age Group Struggles Most

Managing diabetes requires major lifestyle adjustments that are uniquely difficult during adolescence and young adulthood. School pressures, social dynamics, identity formation, and physical change all collide with the demands of a chronic condition requiring daily discipline.

Common Barriers

What Gets in the Way

💸
Cost of Medication
Many patients cannot afford long-term diabetes treatment, especially insulin and newer oral drugs. Since diabetes requires lifelong therapy, financial strain leads to skipped doses, reduced dosing, or complete discontinuation of medication.
📚
Poor Health Literacy
Patients who don't understand diabetes or the importance of strict glycaemic control may not take medication consistently. If symptoms improve, they may wrongly assume they are cured and stop treatment prematurely.
🗓️
Complex Regimens & Busy Schedules
Multiple medications taken at different times, exams, travel, and irregular routines all increase confusion and lead to missed doses. Young adults face unique scheduling challenges that disrupt consistent adherence patterns.
😟
Side Effects & Fear of Injections
Adverse effects like hypoglycaemia, weight gain, or nausea discourage patients from continuing medication. Fear of needles and stigma around insulin injections are major psychological barriers, especially among young adults.
🍕
Social & Dietary Pressures
Balancing social life and dietary restrictions is challenging. Peer and family influences can undermine self-discipline — friends and relatives encouraging indulgence, social events disrupting meal timing and medication schedules.
🙈
Denial & Lack of Long-term Thinking
Some young adults don't fully accept their condition or understand its severity. Without concrete future consequences visible today, it's easy to deprioritise treatment — especially when feeling well in the short term.
Practical Guidance

How to Improve Adherence

For better health outcomes and prevention of complications, here are proven strategies to help you stay on track with your medication.

01
Understand Your Medication
Ask your nurse or doctor what each medication is for, how it works, and what happens if you skip it. Being informed prevents the dangerous assumption that you can stop once you feel better.
💡 Write down your medication names, doses, and timing in your phone's notes app for quick reference.
02
Set Smart Reminders
Set recurring alarms on your phone at the exact times you need to take medication. You can also ask a trusted family member to remind you daily, or use a medication reminder app.
💡 Apps like Medisafe, Roundhealth, and MyTherapy are free and designed specifically for diabetes medication reminders.
03
Make It Part of Your Daily Routine
Link taking your medication to an existing daily habit — before brushing your teeth, at breakfast, or before bed. The stronger the habit anchor, the less effort it takes to remember.
💡 Place your medication visibly next to your toothbrush, coffee maker, or keys as a physical cue.
04
Manage Side Effects Properly
If you experience side effects, inform your healthcare provider — do not simply stop taking the medication. There are often alternative formulations, dosage adjustments, or complementary strategies that address discomfort.
💡 Keep a simple side-effect diary (date, symptom, severity) to share at your next appointment.
05
Never Skip a Dose — Even on Good Days
Regardless of how well you feel, it is critical not to miss any dose. Feeling fine is often proof the medication is working — stopping prematurely disrupts glucose control and can trigger a rapid worsening of your condition.
💡 If you miss a dose, take it as soon as you remember — unless it is almost time for your next dose. Never double up.
06
Refill Before You Run Out
Do not wait until your medication is completely finished to get a refill. Running out creates dangerous gaps in treatment. Keep at least a week's supply as a buffer and schedule refills in advance.
💡 Many pharmacies offer auto-refill subscriptions — ask your pharmacist about this option.
07
Build a Support Network
Talk to family and close friends about your condition and treatment needs. Emotional support goes a long way. Consider joining a diabetes support group — peer connection with others navigating the same challenges is proven to improve adherence.
💡 The International Diabetes Federation (IDF) and local diabetes associations often have peer support programmes for young adults.
